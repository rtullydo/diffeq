Consider the system

\begin{equation*} \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} -3 \amp 1\\ 3 \amp -1 \end{array}\right)\mathbf{x}. \end{equation*}

Find the general solution and draw its Phase portrait.

Solution:

Note \(\det A=0\) then this will always tell us that one of the eigenvalues is \(\lambda_{1}=0\text{.}\) One can easily find that

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{2}=-4. \end{equation*}

After some work, one obtains that

  1. The eigenvalue \(\lambda_{1}=0\) has all eigenvectors on the line \(x_{2}=3x_{1}\text{,}\) so choose \(\mathbf{v}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 3 \end{array}\right)\text{.}\)
  2. The eigenvalue \(\lambda_{1}=-4\) has all eigenvectrs on the line \(x_{1}=-x_{2}\text{,}\) so choose \(\mathbf{v}_{2}=\left(\begin{array}{c} -1\\ 1 \end{array}\right)\text{.}\)

The general solution is given by

\begin{align*} \mathbf{x}(t) \amp = c_{1}e^{0t}\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 3 \end{array}\right)+c_{2}e^{-4t}\left(\begin{array}{c} -1\\ 1 \end{array}\right)\\ \amp = c_{1}\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 3 \end{array}\right)+c_{2}e^{-4t}\left(\begin{array}{c} -1\\ 1 \end{array}\right). \end{align*}

Draw the Phase portrait:

Where are the infinite equilibrium solutions? The line corresponding to the eigenvectors for the zero eigenvalue is where all the equilibrium solutions are located. All other solutions are straight-solutions that either source out or sink in (depending on the sign of \(\lambda_{2}\)). In this example, all the straight-line solutions point inwards because \(\lambda_{2}=-4\text{.}\) The slope of the straight-line solutions are given by direction of the eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_{2}=\left(\begin{array}{c} -1\\ 1 \end{array}\right)\text{.}\)
