A power series converges at \(x_0\) if

\begin{equation*} \lim_{n \to \infty} a_0 + a_1 (x_0 - a) + \ldots + a_n (x_0 -a)^n = \lim_{n\to \infty} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_k (x_0 - a)^k = \lim_{n\to \infty} S_n \text{ exists.} \end{equation*}

The function \(S_n(x)\) is called the \(n\)th partial sum. Often times, we'll use the shorthand notation

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^\infty a_k (x - a)^k = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=0}^n a_k(x-a)^k \end{equation*}

to represent the limit of the partial sums.
