In order to take the inverse of a product, we need to define the convolution integral: Let \(f(t),g(t)\) be two nice functions, then

\begin{equation*} \left(f\star g\right)(t)=\int_{0}^{t}f\left(t-\tau\right)g\left(\tau\right)d\tau=\int_{0}^{t}f(\tau)g\left(t-\tau\right)d\tau. \end{equation*}

The function \(h=f\star g\) is called the \textbf{convolution} of \(f\) and \(g\text{.}\) We can think of convolution as somehow “mixing” or “averaging” the two functions that are being convolved. In practice, convolutions are often used to take jagged, unsmooth functions as input and return smoothed functions as output by convoluting the rough function with an appropriate smooth one.
