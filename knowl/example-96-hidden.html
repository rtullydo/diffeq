Are \(\mathbf{x}=\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 2 \end{array}\right)\) and \(\mathbf{y}=\left(\begin{array}{c} 3\\ 4 \end{array}\right)\) linearly independent?

Method1: Note

\begin{align*} c_{1}\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 2 \end{array}\right)+c_{2}\left(\begin{array}{c} 3\\ 4 \end{array}\right)=0 \amp \iff c_{1}\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 2 \end{array}\right)+c_{2}\left(\begin{array}{c} 3\\ 4 \end{array}\right)=0\\ \amp \iff \left(\begin{array}{c} c_{1}+3c_{2}\\ 2c_{1}+4c_{2} \end{array}\right)=0\\ \amp \iff \left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 \amp 3\\ 2 \amp 4 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c} c_{1}\\ c_{2} \end{array}\right)=0\\ \amp \iff \det\left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 \amp 3\\ 2 \amp 4 \end{array}\right)=4-6\neq 0 \end{align*}

and from TheoremÂ 5.3.1 this tells us that they only solution is the trivial solution \(c_{1}=c_{2}=0\) . Hence yes! linearly independent!

Note that to check linear independence, all we needed to check was if

\begin{equation*} \det\left(\begin{array}{cc} \mathbf{x} \amp \mathbf{y}\end{array}\right)\neq0. \end{equation*}

Method2: Draw this on the \(x-y\) plane and note they're NOT on the same line through the origin. Hence they are linearly independent.
