The total derivative of a function \(z = f(x,y)\) is given by the expression

\begin{equation*} df = f_x \, dx + f_y \, dy. \end{equation*}

Notice that the functions that appear as components in \(df\) are the components of the gradient \(\nabla f\text{.}\) If we were given the equation of an equipotential for \(f\text{,}\) say

\begin{equation*} f(x, y) = C, \end{equation*}

then the total derivative of the equation is

\begin{align*} \amp df = dC \\ \Rightarrow \amp f_x \, dx + f_y \, dy = 0\\ \Rightarrow \amp M \, dx + N \, dy = 0. \end{align*}

If we have further that \(f\) has continuous second partial derivatives on a rectangle \(a \leq x \leq b, c \leq y \leq d\text{,}\) then Clairaut's theorem gives

\begin{equation*} M_y = f_{xy} = f_{yx} = N_x. \end{equation*}
