Our goal is to demonstrate how to find eigenvalues and their corresponding eigenvectors of a given matrix

\begin{equation*} A=\left(\begin{array}{cc} a \amp b\\ c \amp d \end{array}\right). \end{equation*}

First we find the eigenvalues, then we use the eigenvalue to find the corresponding eigenvectors. Let \(I=\left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \end{array}\right)\) be the identity matrix then \(\lambda I=\left(\begin{array}{cc} \lambda \amp 0\\ 0 \amp \lambda \end{array}\right)\text{.}\) Note that by the definition of an eigenvalue, we must have that for some nonzero \(\mathbf{v}\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v} \amp \iff A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}\\ \amp \iff A\mathbf{v}-\lambda\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \\ \amp \iff A\mathbf{v}-\lambda I\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\\ \amp \iff \left(A-\lambda I\right)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}. \end{align*}

Now \(A-\lambda I\) is actually another matrix. What matrix? Let's see,

\begin{align*} A-\lambda I \amp = \left(\begin{array}{cc} a \amp b\\ c \amp d \end{array}\right)-\left(\begin{array}{cc} \lambda \amp 0\\ 0 \amp \lambda \end{array}\right)\\ \amp = \left(\begin{array}{cc} a-\lambda \amp b\\ c \amp d-\lambda \end{array}\right). \end{align*}
