Section 5.1 Power Series

¶

Suppose that we're given the problem

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} = e^{x^2}. \end{equation*}

This is pretty obviously a separable differential equation, and after resorting we get

\begin{equation*} dy = e^{x^2} \,dx, \end{equation*}

and so

\begin{equation*} y = \int e^{x^2} \, dx. \end{equation*}

Unfortunately, the function \(e^{x^2}\) (an enormously useful function that shows up regularly in probability and statistics in the form of the Gaussian or normal distribution), despite being a very nice function, has no closed form antiderivative. That is, there is no function that I can write down for \(y\text{.}\) Can we really not solve a problem like \(\int_0^1 e^{x^2}\, dx\text{?}\)

In calculus, we learn that one approach to a large class of problems like this is to use power series, which are expressions of the form

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^\infty a_k(x - a)^k = a_0 + a_1 (x-a) + a_2 (x-a)^2 + \ldots \end{equation*}

where \(a_0, a_1,\ldots\) are constants. (We'd really like to think of a power series like giant polynomial, but this is isn't always justified...)

A power series converges at \(x_0\) if

\begin{equation*} \lim_{n \to \infty} a_0 + a_1 (x_0 - a) + \ldots + a_n (x_0 -a)^n = \lim_{n\to \infty} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_k (x_0 - a)^k = \lim_{n\to \infty} S_n \text{ exists.} \end{equation*}

The function \(S_n(x)\) is called the \(n\)th partial sum. Often times, we'll use the shorthand notation

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^\infty a_k (x - a)^k = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=0}^n a_k(x-a)^k \end{equation*}

to represent the limit of the partial sums.

The constant \(a\) is called the center of the power series. A power series centered at \(a\) always converges at \(x = a\) (since all but one of the terms is \(0\)). If there is any other point for the series converges, we call it a convergent power series. If a series fails to converge anywhere except at \(x = a\text{,}\) we call it a divergent power series. A series is called absolutely convergent at \(x_0\) if

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^\infty \abs{a_k} \abs{ x_0-a}^k \text{ exists.} \end{equation*}

If a powre series converges absolutely for some \(x = x_0\text{,}\) then it also converges for every value of \(x\) closer to the center \(a\) than \(x_0\) - that is, the series converges for \(\abs{x - a} \lt \abs{x_0 - a}\text{.}\) The proof of this follows from the squeeze theorem and properties of positive series.

\begin{align*} \amp \abs{x - a} \leq \abs{x_0 - a}\\ \amp \Leftrightarrow \sum_{k=0}^n \abs{a_k}\abs{x - a}^k \leq \sum_{k=0}^m \abs{a_k}\abs{x_0 - a}^k\\ \amp \Leftrightarrow \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=0}^n \abs{a_k}\abs{x - a}^k \leq \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=0}^m \abs{a_k}\abs{x_0 - a}^k \end{align*}

and since the larger sum exists, so too must the smaller. This leads to a question of some importance with power series generally - for a given center \(a\text{,}\) what is the largest set around \(a\) on which the series converges? absolutely? The largest number \(\rho\) for which a power series centered at \(a\) converges for every \(x \in (a - \rho, a + \rho)\) (alteratively all \(x\) so that \(\abs{x - a} \lt \rho\)) is called the radius of convergence. How can we find it?

Two of the most powerful tests for convergence we encounter in calculus are the root and ratio tests, both of which work very nicely on power series because power series contain terms of the form \((x-a)^n\text{.}\)

Theorem 5.1.1. Ratio test for convergence.

Let \(\sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k\) be an infinite series, and suppose that the limit

\begin{equation*} L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \abs{\frac{c_{k+1}}{c_k}} \text{ exists.} \end{equation*}

Then the series converges if \(L \lt 1\) and diverges if \(L > 1\text{.}\) If \(L = 1\text{,}\) the test is inconclusive.

Now let's apply the ratio test to a power series to see if we can discover for which values of \(x\) the series converges. Consider the series \(S = \sum_{k=0}^\infty a_x (x - a)^k\text{.}\) We can apply the ratio test by computing the limit (if it exists)

\begin{align*} L \amp = \lim_{k \to \infty} \abs{a_{k+1}(x - a)^{k+1}}{a_k(x -a)^k}\\ \amp = \lim_{k \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}} \abs{x - a}. \end{align*}

Presuming that \(L\) exists, the ratio test says that the series will converge if \(L \lt 1\text{.}\) Let \(A = \lim_{k\to\infty} \abs{\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}}\text{.}\) Then \(L \lt 1\) is equivalent to

\begin{align*} L = \lim_{k\to\infty} \abs{\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}} \abs{x - a} \amp \lt 1\\ A \abs{x - a} \amp \lt 1\\ \abs{x -a} \amp \lt \frac{1}{A} \end{align*}
Theorem 5.1.2.

Let \(\sum a_k (x-a)^k\) be a power series centered at \(a\) so that \(A = \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}\) exists. If \(A = 0\text{,}\) then the radius of convergence for the series is \(\rho = \infty\) (that is, the series converges for every \(x\)). Otherwise, the radius of converge is \(\rho = \frac{1}{A}\text{.}\) (that is, the series converges for \(x \in (a - \rho, a + \rho)\)).

A convergent power series defines a very special kind of function on its interval of convergence. Let

\begin{equation*} f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_k (x - a)^k \end{equation*}

with domain \(\mathcal D = (a -\rho, a+\rho)\text{.}\) The function \(f\) defined this way is called an analytic function. Such functions are extremely nice - they are smooth, they have derivatives to all orders, they can be integrated as many times as one requires. (n.b.: Functions formed from power series are the central object of study in complex analysis, which consequently is a beautiful subject.)

In calculus, we learn a method for deriving power series of a function called Taylor series.

Definition 5.1.3.
If \(f\) is analytic on an open interval \(I\) centered at \(a\text{,}\) then for all \(x \in I\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k; \end{equation*}
that is, \(f\) has a power series centered at \(a\) with \(a_k = \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}\text{.}\)
Finite Taylor series, called Taylor polynomials, are typically excellent approximations of functions near \(x=a\text{.}\)

So why care about this? We can essentially treat Taylor series like “infinitely long polynomials” where they converge - convergent power series can be treated algebraically like functions and are easily integrated and differentiated:

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} f(x) \amp = \frac{d}{dx} \left[\sum_{k=0}^\infty a_k (x-a)^k\right]\\ \amp= \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{d}{dx} a_k (x-a)^k\\ \amp = \sum_{k=0}^\infty k a_k (x-a)^{k-1}\\ \amp = \sum_{k=1}^\infty k a_k (x-a)^{k-1} \hspace{.1in} \text { (first term was 0)}\\ \amp = \sum_{k=0}^\infty (k+1) a_{k+1} (x-a)^k \hspace{.1in} \text{ (re-index)} \end{align*}

Taking another derivative gets us an expression for the second derivative of a power series:

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^2}{dx^2} f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty (k+2)(k+1) a_{k+2} (x-a)^k. \end{equation*}

If \(f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty a_k (x-a)^k\text{,}\) then we have

\begin{gather*} f'(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty (k+1)a_{k+1} (x-a)^k\\ f''(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty (k+2)(k+1) a_{k+2} (x-a)^k \end{gather*}

We should generally have the following basic power series of common functions memorized:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|c} f(x) \amp a \amp \sum a_k (x-a)^k \amp \rho \amp \text{IOC} \\ \hline e^x \amp 0 \amp \sum \frac{x^k}{k!} \amp \infty \amp (-\infty, \infty) \\ \cos x \amp 0 \amp \sum (-1)^k \frac{x^{2k}}{(2k)!} \amp \infty \amp (-\infty, \infty) \\ \sin x \amp 0 \amp \sum (-1)^k \frac{x^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} \amp \infty \amp (-\infty, \infty) \\ \ln (1+x) \amp 0 \amp \sum (-1)^{k+1} \frac{x^k}{k} \amp 1 \amp (-1, 1) \\ \frac{1}{1-x} \amp 0 \amp \sum x^k \amp 1 \amp (-1,1) \end{array} \end{equation*}
Example 5.1.4. Integrate \(e^{x^2}\).

We can now answer the question posed at the beginning of this discussion: since \(e^x = \sum \frac{x^k}{k!}\text{,}\) we get

\begin{equation*} e^{x^2} = \sum \frac{(x^2)^k}{k!} = \sum \frac{x^{2k}}{k!}. \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{align*} \int e^{x^2} \, dx \amp = \int \sum \frac{x^{2k}}{k!}\,dx \\ \amp = \sum \int \frac{x^{2k}}{k!} \, dx \\ \amp = \left[\sum \frac{x^{2k+1}}{(2k+1) k!}\right] + C \end{align*}

This function has no closed form, but the result can be worked with easily (for example, in approximation).

Example 5.1.5. Applying the ratio test.

Find the radius and interval of convergence of the series

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k}{10^k} (x-5)^k. \end{equation*}

To apply the ratio test, we need the terms

\begin{equation*} c_k = \frac{(-1)^k}{10^k}(x-5)^k \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} c_{k+1} = \frac{(-1)^{k+1}}{10^{k+1}}(x-5)^{k+1}. \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{align*} \lim_{k\to \infty} \abs{\frac{c_{k+1}}{c_k}} \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty} \abs{\frac{(-1)^{k+1}(x-5)^{k+1}}{10^{k+1}}\cdot\frac{10^k}{(-1)^k}{(-1)^k(x-5)^k}}\\ \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty} \abs{\frac{1}{10}(x-5)} = \frac{1}{10}\abs{x-5} \end{align*}

which by the ratio test will converge if

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{10}\abs{x-5} \amp \lt 1\\ \text{or } \abs{x-5} \lt 10. \end{align*}

Then the radius of convergence \(\rho = 10\text{,}\) and the interval of converge, which has endpoints given by \(a \pm \rho\text{,}\) is \((-5,15)\text{.}\)
