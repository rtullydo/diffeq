Section 5.7 Repeated and zero eigenvalues

Subsection 5.7.1 Repeated real eigenvalues

Suppose

\begin{equation*} \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 \amp -2\\ 2 \amp 5 \end{array}\right)\mathbf{x}. \end{equation*}

Let's first find the eigenvalues:

\begin{align*} \det\left(\begin{array}{cc} 1-\lambda -2\\ 2 \amp 5-\lambda \end{array}\right)=0 \amp \iff \left(1-\lambda\right)\left(5-\lambda\right)+4=0\\ \amp \iff \lambda^{2}-6\lambda+9=0\\ \amp \iff \left(\lambda-3\right)^{2}=0,\\ \amp \iff \lambda=3,3. \end{align*}

Based on the intuition we've built from second order ODEs, in Section 3.5, it is reasonable to guess that the solution to this system will be of the form

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{x}(t)=e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{0}+te^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{1}. \end{equation*}

where \(\mathbf{x}(0)=\mathbf{v}_{0}\) is the initial condition. So what will \(\mathbf{v}_{1}\) be? Let's try figure it out. For this solution to work, we check that when plugging this \(\mathbf{x}\) into

\begin{equation*} \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}=A\mathbf{x}, \end{equation*}

the Left-hand-side (LHS) equals to Right-hand-side(RHS) of the equation.

First let's compute the LHS, when plugging our guess into the equation,

\begin{align*} \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} \amp = \frac{d}{dt}\left(e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{0}+te^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{1}\right)\\ \amp = \lambda e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{0}+e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{1}+\lambda te^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{1}\\ \amp = \left(\lambda\mathbf{v}_{0}+\mathbf{v}_{1}\right)e^{\lambda t}+\left(\lambda\mathbf{v}_{1}\right)te^{\lambda t} \end{align*}

Now we plug our guess into the RHS,

\begin{align*} A\mathbf{x} \amp = A\left(e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{0}+te^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{1}\right)\\ \amp = \left(A\mathbf{v}_{0}\right)e^{\lambda t}+\left(A\mathbf{v}_{1}\right)te^{\lambda t} \end{align*}

So matching the coefficients of \(e^{\lambda t}\) and \(te^{\lambda t}\) in the LHS and RHS, we get that

\begin{equation*} \lambda\mathbf{v}_{1}=A\mathbf{v}_{1}\mbox{ and }\lambda\mathbf{v}_{0}+\mathbf{v}_{1}=A\mathbf{v}_{0}. \end{equation*}

The first equation says that \(\mathbf{v}_{1}\) is either an eigenvector or the zero vector. But also, it must satisfy the second equation. Thus, by solving for $\mathbf{v}_{1}$ in the second equation, we get that

\begin{align*} \lambda\mathbf{v}_{0}+\mathbf{v}_{1}=A\mathbf{v}_{0} \amp \iff \mathbf{v}_{1}=A\mathbf{v}_{0}-\lambda\mathbf{v}_{0}\\ \amp \iff \mathbf{v}_{1}=\left(A-\lambda I\right)\mathbf{v}_{0}. \end{align*}

Thus we summarize these computations in a theorem.

Theorem 5.7.1. General Solution for repeated real eigenvalues.

Suppose \(\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}=A\mathbf{x}\) is a system of which \(\lambda\) is a repeated real eigenvalue. Then the general solution is of the form:

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{x}(t)=e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{0}+te^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{1} \end{equation*}

where

\begin{align*} \mathbf{v}_{0} \amp =\mathbf{x}(0)\text{ (initial condition)}\\ \mathbf{v}_{1} \amp =\left(A-\lambda I\right)\mathbf{v}_{0}. \end{align*}

Moreover, if \(\mathbf{v}_{1}\neq\mathbf{0}\) then it is an eigenvector with eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{,}\) and if \(\mathbf{v}_{1}=0\) then \(\mathbf{v}_{0}\) is an eigenvector and \(\mathbf{x}(t)\) is a straight-line solution.

Warning.

Never think that \(e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{0},te^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_{1}\) are generally solutions by themselves. This method is different than our usual method of finding two linearly independent solutions \(\mathbf{x}^{(1)}\) and \(\mathbf{x}^{(2)}\text{.}\)

We can now do an example.

Example 5.7.2.

Find the solution to the following IVP:

\begin{equation*} \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 \amp -2\\ 2 \amp 5 \end{array}\right)\mathbf{x},\,\,\,\,\,\mathbf{x}(0)=\left(\begin{array}{c} 2\\ 1 \end{array}\right). \end{equation*}

Then graph the phase portrait for the system and on a separate graph plot the particular solution to the IVP above.

Solution:

Step 1: Recall we found \(\lambda=3\) to be an eigenvalue in the discusion in the beginning of the section. We repeat the calculation here:

\begin{align*} \det\left(\begin{array}{cc} 1-\lambda -2\\ 2 \amp 5-\lambda \end{array}\right)=0 \amp \iff \left(1-\lambda\right)\left(5-\lambda\right)+4=0\\ \amp \iff \lambda^{2}-6\lambda+9=0\\ \amp \iff \left(\lambda-3\right)^{2}=0,\\ \amp \iff \lambda=3,3. \end{align*}

Step 2: Suppose \(\mathbf{v}_{0}=\left(\begin{array}{c} x_{0}\\ y_{0} \end{array}\right)\) is the initial condition. Then we can compute \(\mathbf{v}_{1}\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} \mathbf{v}_{1} \amp = \left(A-\lambda I\right)\mathbf{v}_{0}\\ \amp = \left(\begin{array}{cc} -2 \amp -2\\ 2 \amp 2 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c} x_{0}\\ y_{0} \end{array}\right)\\ \amp = \left(\begin{array}{c} -2x_{0}-2y_{0}\\ 2x_{0}+2y_{0} \end{array}\right). \end{align*}

Step 3: Write the general solution

\begin{align*} \mathbf{x}(t) \amp = e^{3t}\mathbf{v}_{0}+te^{3t}\mathbf{v}_{1}\\ \amp = e^{3t}\left(\begin{array}{c} x_{0}\\ y_{0} \end{array}\right)+te^{\lambda3t}\left(\begin{array}{c} -2x_{0}-2y_{0}\\ 2x_{0}+2y_{0} \end{array}\right) \end{align*}

and plugging the inital condition we have

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{x}(t)=e^{3t}\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 2 \end{array}\right)+te^{3t}\left(\begin{array}{c} -6\\ 6 \end{array}\right). \end{equation*}

Step4: We now make a phase portrait for the system. We plot the solutions by plotting the straight line solutions first (\(y=-x\)) which is associated to the eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and then making the following graphs (an almost spiral, or more formally an asymptotically unstable improper node) with arrows pointing outwards (because \(\lambda=3>0\)). We call this an almost spiral, because solutions look like they are trying spiral around the equilibrium point \(\mathbf{0}\text{,}\) but there's are straight-line solution blocking the other solutions from going around the origin. You should still check if the direction of the almost spirals are clockwise/counterclockwise .

Subsection 5.7.2 Systems with a zero eigenvalue (\(\det A=0 \))

Recall that when \(\det A=0\) then by Theorem 5.3.1, we must have infinitely many equilibrium solutions. Note that in all examples we've worked with so far, the only equilibrium solution was the trivial equilibrium solution, which means we've only worked with systems where \(\det A\neq0\text{.}\) It turns out that when \(\det A=0\) then there will always exist be a zero eigenvalue, say

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{1}=0. \end{equation*}
Proposition 5.7.3.

Let \(A\) be a \(2\times 2\) matrix. If \(\det A=0\text{,}\) then one of the eigenvalues will be \(0\text{.}\)

Proof.

We leave this as an exercise.

Now when \(\det A=0\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{1}=0\,\,\,\,\,\lambda_{1}=\text{ some other real number}, \end{equation*}

with corresponding eigenvectors \(\mathbf{v}_{1}\) and \(\mathbf{v}_{2}\text{,}\) respectively. Let's assume for now that \(\lambda_{2}\) is distinct from \(\lambda_{1}\text{.}\) Then we are back at the distinct, real eigenvalue case, and hence everything from Section 5.4 still hold. Thus the general solution to

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{x}^{\prime}=A\mathbf{x} \end{equation*}

is given by

\begin{align*} \mathbf{x}(t) \amp =c_{1}e^{\lambda_{1}t}\mathbf{v}_{1}+c_{2}e^{\lambda_{2}t}\mathbf{v}_{2}\\ \amp =c_{1}e^{0}\mathbf{v}_{1}+c_{2}e^{\lambda_{2}t}\mathbf{v}_{2}\\ \amp =c_{1}\mathbf{v}_{1}+c_{2}e^{\lambda_{2}t}\mathbf{v}_{2}. \end{align*}

The only main difference is that the Phase portrait will look different, since now there are infinitely many equilibrium solutions for this system. In fact, all solutions are either straight line solutions, or are equilibrium solutions. We describe this case, in the following example.

Example 5.7.4.

Consider the system

\begin{equation*} \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} -3 \amp 1\\ 3 \amp -1 \end{array}\right)\mathbf{x}. \end{equation*}

Find the general solution and draw its Phase portrait.

Solution:

Note \(\det A=0\) then this will always tell us that one of the eigenvalues is \(\lambda_{1}=0\text{.}\) One can easily find that

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{2}=-4. \end{equation*}

After some work, one obtains that

  1. The eigenvalue \(\lambda_{1}=0\) has all eigenvectors on the line \(x_{2}=3x_{1}\text{,}\) so choose \(\mathbf{v}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 3 \end{array}\right)\text{.}\)
  2. The eigenvalue \(\lambda_{1}=-4\) has all eigenvectrs on the line \(x_{1}=-x_{2}\text{,}\) so choose \(\mathbf{v}_{2}=\left(\begin{array}{c} -1\\ 1 \end{array}\right)\text{.}\)

The general solution is given by

\begin{align*} \mathbf{x}(t) \amp = c_{1}e^{0t}\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 3 \end{array}\right)+c_{2}e^{-4t}\left(\begin{array}{c} -1\\ 1 \end{array}\right)\\ \amp = c_{1}\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 3 \end{array}\right)+c_{2}e^{-4t}\left(\begin{array}{c} -1\\ 1 \end{array}\right). \end{align*}

Draw the Phase portrait:

Where are the infinite equilibrium solutions? The line corresponding to the eigenvectors for the zero eigenvalue is where all the equilibrium solutions are located. All other solutions are straight-solutions that either source out or sink in (depending on the sign of \(\lambda_{2}\)). In this example, all the straight-line solutions point inwards because \(\lambda_{2}=-4\text{.}\) The slope of the straight-line solutions are given by direction of the eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_{2}=\left(\begin{array}{c} -1\\ 1 \end{array}\right)\text{.}\)
