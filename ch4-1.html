Section 4.1 Linear equations

¶

Subsection 4.1.1 General linear equations

Everything we did in Chapter 3 for second order linear equations can be extended to higher order systems. Suppose we have the \(n\)th order linear equation

\begin{equation*} a_{n}(t)y^{(n)}+a_{n-1}(t)y^{(n-1)}+\cdots+a_{1}(t)y^{\prime}+a_{0}(t)y=g(t). \end{equation*}

We assume that \(a_{n}(t),\dots,a_{0}(t)\) are continuous functions on an interval \(I\text{,}\) and that \(a_{n}(t)\neq0\) inside the interval: so that we can write it in standand form as

\begin{equation*} y^{(n)}+p_{n-1}(t)y^{(n-1)}+\cdots+p_{1}(t)y^{\prime}+p_{0}(t)y=g(t).\,\,\,\,\,(\star) \end{equation*}

with initial conditions

\begin{equation*} y(t_{0})=y_{0},\,\,y^{\prime}(t_{0})=y_{0}^{\prime},\,\,\cdots,y^{(n-1)}(t_{0})=y_{0}^{(n-1)}.\,\,\,\,\,(\star)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Theorem 4.1.1. Existence/uniqueness.

Let a linear differential equation be given in form \((\star)\text{.}\) If \(p_{n-1}(t),\dots,p_{0}(t)\) are continuous functions on an open interval \(I\) (containing \(t_{0}\)), then there exists a unique solution \(y=\phi(t)\) throughout all of \(I\) to the IVP in \((\star)\text{.}\)

Example 4.1.2.

Consider the ODE

\begin{equation*} (t-2)y^{(4)}+\sin ty^{\prime\prime\prime}+\ln ty=\sqrt{t+5}. \end{equation*}

Find the intervals where you are guaranteed a unique solution to this ODE by the Uniqueness and Existence Theorem.

Rewriting, we have

\begin{equation*} y^{(4)}+\frac{\sin t}{(t-2)}y^{\prime\prime\prime}+\frac{\ln t}{(t-2)}y=\frac{\sqrt{t+5}}{(t-2)} \end{equation*}

and

  • \(\frac{\sin t}{(t-2)}\) is comtinuous when \(t\neq2\)
  • \(\frac{\ln t}{(t-2)}\) is continuous when \(t>0\) and \(t\neq2\text{,}\) and
  • \(\frac{\sqrt{t+5}}{(t-2)}\) is continuous when \(t\geq-5\) and \(t\neq2\text{.}\)

Making a number line we see that all three functions are continuous when either on the interval \((0,2)\) or \((2,\infty)\text{.}\)

Subsection 4.1.2 Constant coefficients

Now consider the homogeneous \(n\)th order linear equation with constant coefficients.

\begin{equation*} a_{n}y^{(n)}+a_{n-1}y^{(n-1)}+\cdots+a_{1}y^{\prime}+a_{0}y=0. \end{equation*}

As we did in the \(2\)nd order case, the first thing we do is guess that the solution will look like \(y=e^{rt}\) and

\begin{align*} y \amp =e^{rt}\\ y^{\prime} \amp =re^{rt},\\ \amp \vdots\\ y^{(n)} \amp =r^{n}e^{rt}. \end{align*}

Plugging into the LHS and setting equal to zero we have

\begin{align*} LHS \amp =a_{n}\left(r^{n}e^{rt}\right)+\cdots+a_{1}\left(re^{rt}\right)+a_{0}\left(e^{rt}\right)\\ \amp =e^{rt}\left(a_{n}r^{n}+\cdots+a_{0}\right)\\ \amp =RHS=0 \end{align*}

hence

\begin{equation*} e^{rt}\left(a_{n}r^{n}+\cdots+a_{0}\right)=0. \end{equation*}

But since \(e^{rt}\neq0\) then

\begin{equation*} a_{n}r^{n}+\cdots+a_{1}r+a_{0}=0. \end{equation*}

As before the characteristic equation is given by:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c} \underbrace{a_{n}r^{n}+\cdots+a_{0}}\\ Z(r) \end{array}=0, \end{equation*}

where we call \(Z(r)\) the characteristic polynomial. How do we solve \(n-\)degree polynomials? By factoring! The fundamental theorem of algebra guarantees that an \(n\)th degree polynomial factors into \(n\) linear terms (assuming that we allow complex roots).

\begin{equation*} Z(t)=a_{n}\left(r-r_{1}\right)\left(r-r_{2}\right)\cdots\left(r-r_{n}\right). \end{equation*}

HOWEVER, there is no general approach to factoring polynomials of degree greater than 4. Numerical techniques are necessary in these cases.

Solutions to the ODE are built exactly like in the 2nd degree case. If there are any repeat solutions, then keep multiplying by \(t\) until you don't have any more repeat solutions.

Example 4.1.3.

Find general solution and the particular solution to the IVP

\begin{equation*} y^{\prime\prime\prime}-2y^{\prime\prime}-y^{\prime}+2y=0.\,\,\,\,y(0)=0,y^{\prime}(0)=1,y^{\prime}(0)=2. \end{equation*}

(Hint: Suppose you know \(r^{3}-2r^{2}-r+2=\left(r-2\right)\left(r+1\right)\left(r-1\right)\))

The characteristic equation is \(2r^{3}-4r^{2}-2r+4=0\) and by the hint we have

\begin{equation*} \left(r-2\right)\left(r+1\right)\left(r-1\right)=0, \end{equation*}

hence the general solution is \(y(t)=c_{1}e^{2t}+c_{2}e^{-t}+c_{3}e^{t}\text{.}\) To find the particular solution to the IVP we start by:

\begin{align*} y(t) \amp =c_{1}e^{2t}+c_{2}e^{-t}+c_{3}e^{t}\\ y^{\prime}(t) \amp =2c_{1}e^{2t}-c_{2}e^{-t}+c_{3}e^{t}\\ y^{\prime\prime}(t) \amp =4c_{1}e^{2t}+c_{2}e^{-t}+c_{2}e^{t}. \end{align*}

Then we have to solve the following system of equations:

\begin{align*} 0 \amp =c_{1}+c_{2}+c_{3}\\ 1 \amp =2c_{1}-c_{2}+c_{3}\\ 2 \amp =4c_{1}+c_{2}+c_{3} \end{align*}

and get \(c_{1}=\frac{2}{3}\text{,}\) \(c_{2}=-\frac{1}{6}\) and \(c_{3}=-\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) hence

\begin{equation*} y(t)=\frac{2}{3}e^{2t}-\frac{1}{6}e^{-t}-\frac{1}{2}e^{t}. \end{equation*}
Example 4.1.4.

Find general solution of

\begin{equation*} y^{(4)}+8y^{\prime\prime\prime}+16y^{\prime\prime}=0. \end{equation*}

(Hint: \(r^{4}+8r^{3}+16r^{2}=r^{2}\left(r+4\right)^{2}\))

The characteristic polynomial is

\begin{align*} r^{4}+8r^{3}+16r^{2} \amp =0 \end{align*}

which by the hint we know factors as

\begin{equation*} r^{2}\left(r+4\right)^{2}=0. \end{equation*}

Note that since this a 4th degree polynomial we need to have 4 roots: \(0,0,-4,-4\text{.}\) So we use the same method we do when we have repeats and get

\begin{align*} y(t) \amp =c_{1}e^{0t}+c_{2}te^{0t}+c_{3}e^{-4t}+c_{4}te^{-4t}\\ \amp =c_{1}+c_{2}t+c_{3}e^{-4t}+c_{4}te^{-4t}. \end{align*}
Example 4.1.5.

Solve

\begin{equation*} y^{(4)}+y^{\prime\prime\prime}-5y^{\prime\prime}+y^{\prime}-6y=0. \end{equation*}

(Hint: Suppose \(\left(r-2\right)\left(r+3\right)\left(r^{2}+1\right)\))

The characteristic equation is given by

\begin{equation*} r^{4}+r^{3}-5r^{2}+r-6=0 \end{equation*}

and by the hint

\begin{equation*} Z(r)=\left(r-2\right)\left(r+3\right)\left(r^{2}+1\right)=0 \end{equation*}

which gives

\begin{equation*} r=2,-3,\pm i \end{equation*}

hence

\begin{equation*} y(t)=c_{1}e^{2t}+c_{2}e^{-3t}+c_{3}\cos t+c_{3}\sin t. \end{equation*}
Example 4.1.6.

Solve

\begin{equation*} y^{\prime\prime\prime}-3y^{\prime\prime}+3y^{\prime}-y=0. \end{equation*}

(Hint: \(r^{3}-3r^{2}+3r-1=\left(r-1\right)\left(r-1\right)^{2}\))

The characteristic polynomial is \(r^{3}-3r^{2}+3r-1=0\) and by the hint,

\begin{equation*} \left(r-1\right)^{3}=0 \end{equation*}

\item So that \(r=1,1,1\)

\begin{equation*} y(t)=c_{1}e^{t}+c_{2}te^{t}+c_{3}t^{2}e^{t}. \end{equation*}
Example 4.1.7.

Solve

\begin{equation*} y^{(4)}+8y^{\prime\prime}-9y=0 \end{equation*}

(Hint: \(r^{4}+8r^{2}-9=\left(r^{2}-1\right)\left(r^{2}+9\right)\))

By the hint

\begin{align*} r^{4}+8r^{2}-9 \amp =\left(r^{2}-1\right)\left(r^{2}+9\right)\\ \amp =\left(r-1\right)\left(r+1\right)\left(r-3i\right)\left(r+3i\right). \end{align*}

then

\begin{equation*} y(t)=c_{1}e^{t}+c_{2}e^{-t}+c_{3}\cos(3t)+c_{4}\sin(3t). \end{equation*}
Example 4.1.8.

Suppose the roots of the characteristic equation are

\begin{equation*} 2,3,3,3,2\pm3i,2\pm3i \end{equation*}

then the general solution is

\begin{align*} y(t) \amp =c_{1}e^{2t}+c_{2}e^{3t}+c_{3}te^{3t}+c_{4}t^{2}e^{3t}\\ \amp +c_{5}e^{2t}\cos(3t)+c_{6}e^{2t}\sin(3t)\\ \amp +c_{5}te^{2t}\cos(3t)+c_{6}te^{2t}\sin(3t). \end{align*}
